**P-02 | Career Paths Are Rarely Straight Lines**

Today, while scrolling through LinkedIn, I came across a post about a laboratory project.

There was nothing particularly remarkable about it.

A few site photos, a floor plan, and a short project description.

Yet I still stopped and read the entire post.

Afterwards, I found myself thinking about something.

Looking back, my career path has not unfolded exactly the way I once imagined.

If someone had told me twenty years ago that there would be a period when I stepped away from laboratory projects and found myself working in completely different environments, I probably would not have believed them.

Because that was never part of the plan.

But careers do not always follow plans.

Some decisions are driven by goals.

Others are shaped by reality.

Sometimes we simply deal with what is in front of us before we can continue searching for the direction we truly want to pursue.

What I find interesting is that, although my work has changed over the years, some habits never really did.

Whenever I come across topics related to laboratory planning, operational workflows, system coordination, or project delivery, I still find myself clicking in for a closer look.

Sometimes I want to understand why a layout was organized a certain way.

Sometimes I am curious about how a particular challenge was resolved.

Sometimes a single drawing is enough to send my thoughts down a familiar path of analysis and exploration.

Only recently did I begin to realize what that might mean.

Perhaps what defines us is not only what we are doing today.

It is also the things we continue to pay attention to, continue to think about, and continue to invest our time in, even after life has taken us elsewhere.

Career paths are rarely straight lines.

There are detours.

There are pauses.

There are times when it feels as though we have ended up on an entirely different track.

But every now and then, something catches your attention and reminds you that the direction you truly wanted to pursue never really changed.

For me, laboratory projects, system coordination, and the operational logic behind complex environments remain that direction.

And that is why, over the past while, I have been making a conscious effort to reconnect with this field.

Not because I am looking backward.

But because I have become increasingly certain about where I want to go next.

So when I finished reading that project post, I realized that what held my attention was not the project itself.

It was the reminder.

Some directions may temporarily drift out of sight.

That does not mean they have been abandoned.

And some paths, no matter how far they wander, still have a way of leading us back to where we always intended to be.
