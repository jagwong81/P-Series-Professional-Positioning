# P-03 | Before Looking for Solutions

Two questions have been on my mind recently.

Both came from the same interview.

One was about a team.

The other was about a client.

> "If someone on your team suddenly became disengaged, stopped cooperating, and their enthusiasm for work noticeably declined, what would you do?"

> "If a client became furious because the project schedule had fallen seriously behind, how would you respond?"

At first glance, the two questions had very little in common. One was about people; the other was about projects.

Yet, without really thinking about it, both of my answers started in exactly the same place—not with the solution, but with understanding the situation.

---

After enough years working on projects, I've found that solutions are rarely the difficult part.

Communication, coordination, temporary role changes, additional support, time away from work, escalation, recovery plans... experience naturally gives us more options.

Finding *something* to do has never seemed particularly difficult.

Deciding **whether this is the right time to do it** usually takes much longer.

---

A team member losing motivation doesn't immediately make me think about having a conversation.

Before deciding how to respond, I'd rather understand what actually happened.

- Is it temporary, or has it been building for some time?
- Is it affecting only one person, or beginning to affect the rest of the team?
- Did it begin at work, or somewhere outside work?

Without understanding where the problem begins, every solution risks becoming little more than temporary relief.

---

The same thought stayed with me when I came to the client question.

Before thinking about apologies or explanations, there were other questions that felt more important.

- Was the original schedule realistic?
- How serious was the delay?
- Where did the responsibility actually lie?
- If it was ours, how much of it was ours?

Those answers wouldn't solve the problem by themselves.

But they would determine what the next conversation should look like.

---

One part of my answer probably says more about me than anything else.

As long as there is still room to help, there is still a responsibility to try.

I don't like sending a teammate to a place where I no longer have any influence over what happens next until every reasonable effort has already been made.

The same applies to clients.

Taking responsibility has never meant saying *"I'm sorry"* as quickly as possible.

It means understanding the situation well enough to return with something more useful than an apology.

---

Looking back, I don't think experience has given me dramatically better solutions over the years.

Most of the solutions were already there.

What has changed is something much quieter.

> Not what to do.
>
> **But when to do it.**
